Sales Process & Qualification (MEDDPICC)

Structured qualification framework. Each stage requires specific evidence before advancing to next phase.

Stage Question to Ask What Good Looks Like Red Flags
M
Metrics		 What does success look like? How do you measure it? Specific metrics: MTTR <30m, alert reduction ±40%, compliance pass within 6 months, cost savings $X Vague answers ("better security"), no budget allocated, no executive alignment on ROI
E
Economic Buyer		 Who owns the security or IT budget? Do they support this initiative? CISO or CFO confirmed as decision-maker; they've expressed interest in conversation; budget cycle known Only technical buyer available, budget owner skeptical, decision deferred indefinitely
D
Decision Criteria		 How will you decide between vendors? What matters most? Written evaluation criteria shared; clear scoring rubric (features, price, support, integration) "We'll know it when we see it," no formal RFP, criteria keep changing mid-evaluation
D
Decision Process		 Walk me through your approval process. How long does it take? Clear timeline: vendor selection by [date], approval by [date], deployment by [date] Process unclear, timeline "to be determined," multiple layers of governance, no deadline
P
Pain		 What's keeping you up at night? What's the cost of inaction? Specific incidents, quantified impact ($X per breach), regulatory pressure (upcoming audit), team burnout No sense of urgency, pain is "nice to have," pain shared only by one stakeholder
P
Pitch		 What message resonates with your team? How do we position this? Buyer confirms our positioning aligns with their needs; specific language they use Buyer silent or says "that won't fly here," competing narrative from another vendor takes hold
I
Influencers		 Who else needs to be consulted? Who are the skeptics? Map of all stakeholders identified; roles and positions mapped; skeptics engaged early Unknown stakeholders emerge late; one person can block decision; influencers won't return calls
C
Champion		 Who is your internal advocate? Will they fight for you? Economic or technical buyer actively championing deal; has credibility with decision-maker No internal advocate, key champion leaves organization, champion loses credibility

Competitive Battlecards

Three primary competitive threats and our counter-positioning.

vs. CrowdStrike

Their Pitch
"Single platform security. Zero trust. One agent, one console, complete visibility."
Their Weakness
Endpoint-centric (doesn't cover cloud, network, API threats). Expensive. Requires their agent everywhere. Hard to integrate with non-CrowdStrike tooling.
Our Counter
We're cloud-native and agnostic. We see threats across endpoints, cloud, API, and network—without requiring agent. Better for multi-vendor shops. Lower cost per threat detected.
Trap Question
"If you go all-in on CrowdStrike, what happens to your existing Splunk/Sumo investment?"

vs. Palo Alto Networks

Their Pitch
"Unified security operations platform. Cortex. Cloud-scale threat intelligence."
Their Weakness
Costly ($500K+ enterprise deals). Slow to deploy (3–6 months). Requires Palo Alto firewall lock-in. Complex licensing. Poor UX for SOC analysts.
Our Counter
Faster deployment (2 weeks). Work with any firewall. Transparent pricing. Built for analysts, not just CISOs. Better automation and response playbooks.
Trap Question
"How many months has Palo Alto taken to deploy in similar organizations?"

vs. Build In-House

Their Pitch
"We can build this ourselves with ELK/Splunk + custom scripts and save money."
Their Weakness
Hidden costs (engineering headcount, 18+ month delivery, maintenance burden, talent retention). Security gaps in custom code. No threat intelligence. Team distracted from core business.
Our Counter
We've already built this. Out-of-box threat detection, playbooks, and intelligence. Your team stays focused on business. Lower TCO. Faster time to value.
Trap Question
"Can you allocate 2 FTEs for the next 18 months? What's the true cost of that vs. our annual platform fee?"

Discovery Call Script

Eight structured questions organized by MEDDPICC stage. Use follow-ups to dig into objections.

  • What's driving the conversation around security tooling right now?
    Listen for pain (breach, audit, tool sprawl, team burnout). If generic, push: "Is there a specific incident or compliance deadline?"
  • How is your security team currently handling threat detection and response?
    Understand current tools, team size, MTTR, alert fatigue level. Dig: "On average, how long from alert to resolution?"
  • What would need to change for this to be a priority this year?
    Uncover decision criteria and timeline. "Is there a budget cycle we should align with?"
  • Who else is involved in the security tooling decision?
    Map the committee: CISO, CFO, CTO, Security Engineering Manager. Ask: "Who would be the strongest advocate for change?"
  • If we could cut your MTTR by half and reduce alert noise by 40%, what would that mean for your team?
    Test value messaging. Watch for resonance. Adjust pitch based on response.
  • What does your current vendor evaluation process look like?
    RFP? Bake-off? Timeline? "When do you hope to make a decision?"
  • Are there any must-haves or deal-breakers we should know about?
    Uncover non-negotiables: integration, uptime SLA, data residency, compliance. "What would disqualify a vendor?"
  • What's the best next step? Would a technical demo or customer reference call be helpful?
    Advance the deal. "Who should attend the demo?" Get budget confirmation: "Are we in scope from a budget perspective?"
